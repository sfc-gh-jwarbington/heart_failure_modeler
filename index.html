Why Multimodal?

⚠

Structured-Only Models: AUC 0.59–0.65

LACE and HOSPITAL scores using only labs, vitals, and diagnosis codes have reached their predictive ceiling. (Frizzell et al., JAMA Cardiology 2017)

📈

+ NLP Clinical Notes: AUC 0.68–0.74

Adding NLP-extracted features from discharge summaries and nursing notes improves discrimination significantly. (Rajkomar et al., npj Digital Medicine 2018)

🚀

Full Multimodal: AUC 0.75–0.82

Combining structured + NLP + SDoH + imaging + temporal + post-discharge signals achieves state-of-the-art performance. (Mortazavi et al., Circ CQO 2016)

Mortality vs. Readmission — The Tension: CMS measures both 30-day mortality (MORT-30-HF) and 30-day readmission (READM-30-HF) independently. Research shows an inverse correlation: hospitals that discharge sicker patients later (reducing mortality) may see higher readmission rates, while those discharging earlier may show lower readmissions but higher mortality. RWJBH should optimize both metrics together, targeting GDMT adherence, transition-of-care programs, and early follow-up. State Readmission Case Studies

Comparative analysis of high- and low-readmission states to contextualize New Jersey and RWJBH's position. Data from Definitive Healthcare 2025, HCUP, CMS, and peer-reviewed literature.

Massachusetts

15.3% — Highest

Why high: Oldest population cohort in New England. Dense Medicare market — among top 5 states in Medicare discharges. Dominated by large academic medical centers (Mass General, Brigham & Women's) treating high-acuity, complex patients from multi-state referral base.

Paradox: Despite nation-leading healthcare quality reputation and near-universal insurance coverage (RomneyCare), state faces highest readmission rate. 93% of MA hospitals face HRRP penalties.

Bright spot: New England Baptist Hospital achieves only 11.7% readmission — demonstrating that targeted discharge planning and outpatient networks work even in high-risk environments.

Sources: Definitive Healthcare 2025; Empire Center; Becker's Hospital Review

New Jersey (RWJBH)

~14.7% — Above Avg

Profile: Mid-Atlantic state with high Medicare volume, dense urban population, and large academic hospital systems. Horwitz et al. found NY/NJ region has ~1 percentage point higher risk-adjusted readmissions than Mountain states.

RWJBH context: 12 hospitals ranging from 18.9% (RWJUH, best) to 22.1% (Jersey City MC, highest risk population). Newark Beth Israel at 21.4% reflects its role as a tertiary referral center for the sickest HF patients (LVAD, transplant, ECMO).

Opportunity: System-wide standardization of discharge planning, medication reconciliation, and 7-day follow-up protocols could drive RWJBH below the state average. Cross-hospital readmission tracking via NJHIN/HIE is critical.

Sources: CMS Care Compare; Horwitz 2017 JGIM; Definitive Healthcare

Idaho

13.3% — Lowest

Why low: Younger population with lower chronic disease prevalence. Smaller hospital systems with moderate volumes. Strong primary care physician density (especially family medicine). Tight-knit community care model with high outpatient continuity.

Key factor: Medicaid expansion in 2018 improved post-discharge coverage for working-age adults. Telehealth adoption in rural areas (accelerated by COVID) enables remote follow-up for discharged patients in distant towns.

Exemplar: St. Luke's Boise Medical Center at 11.5% readmission — one of the lowest in the nation. Strong rural telehealth infrastructure and community health worker programs.

Sources: Definitive Healthcare 2025; CDC state health data

West Virginia

≥15.0% — High

Why high: Oldest median age in the US with rampant cardiovascular and respiratory disease. Highest rates of diabetes, COPD, and obesity nationally. High poverty, low physician density, minimal public transportation.

Systemic barriers: Limited SNF beds and post-acute care infrastructure in Appalachian communities. Widespread Medicaid capacity issues leading to return ED visits instead of planned follow-up. Low primary care ratio compounds chronic disease management gaps.

Efforts: WV DHHR deploying care coordinators; telemedicine growing. However, entrenched disease burden means improvements are slow. Basu et al. (2015) specifically identified WV-like chronic disease clusters as drivers of elevated readmissions.

Sources: CDC state data; Basu 2015 HSR; WV DHHR

Nevada

≥15.0% — High

Why high: Heavy chronic disease burden (smoking, obesity, heart disease). Large safety-net hospital systems in Las Vegas serving Medicaid and uninsured populations. Significant homeless and behavioral health populations cycling through hospitals.

Structural issues: Rural Northern NV has extremely limited post-acute care options. Many for-profit hospitals (Horwitz et al. found +0.38% higher readmission rates at for-profit facilities). No statewide readmission reduction program. Social services strained.

Comparison to NJ: Both states share for-profit hospital influence and urban safety-net challenges. Nevada lacks NJ's academic medical center infrastructure and robust HIE network (NJHIN), suggesting RWJBH has structural advantages to exploit.

Sources: Horwitz 2017 JGIM; Definitive Healthcare; IntuitionLabs 2026

Lessons for RWJBH

Key Takeaways

1. Geography is not destiny: MA's New England Baptist (11.7%) and ID's St. Luke's (11.5%) prove that individual hospitals can dramatically outperform their state average with targeted programs.

2. Community factors matter: PCP supply, SNF availability, and social vulnerability indices explain substantial readmission variance beyond clinical care (Jacobs 2022, Vizient 2025).

3. Cross-hospital tracking is essential: 25%+ of readmissions occur at a different facility ($21B cost). RWJBH's NJHIN/HIE integration gives it a data advantage most systems lack.

4. Payer mix shift coming: FY2027 MA data inclusion will change the competitive landscape. RWJBH should prepare by including MA beneficiaries in readmission analytics now.

5. Model must include system-level factors: The 7 new system-level risk factors (PCP supply, SNF beds, payer mix, etc.) capture dimensions that pure clinical models miss.

Data Sources: Definitive Healthcare State Readmissions 2025 • IntuitionLabs State Analysis 2026 • CMS Care Compare • Horwitz 2017 • Jacobs 2022

Model Development Roadmap

⚫ IN REVIEW

Phase 1: Cardiologist Review of Risk Factors

Clinical review of 91 candidate risk factors by cardiology team. Each factor evaluated for clinical relevance, evidence strength, data type, and code set alignment. Inclusion/exclusion decisions documented with priority ranking. Approved factor list serves as the feature specification for model development.

Deliverable: Approved risk factor list with strength ratings, priority order, and code set mappings (CSV export available above).

▶ IN PROGRESS

Phase 2: Snowflake Data Element Scan & Gap Analysis

Systematic scan of RWJBH Snowflake data warehouse (Epic Clarity/Caboodle, claims, ADT feeds) to map each approved risk factor to available data elements. Identifies which factors have structured data readily available, which require NLP extraction from clinical notes, and which need external data source enrichment (e.g., SDoH indices, census data, pharmacy fills).

Deliverable: Data availability matrix — each factor mapped to Snowflake table/column, coverage %, and gap remediation plan.

Phase 3: Model Create, Train & Test Cycles

Iterative model development in Snowflake using Snowpark ML and Snowflake Model Registry. Feature engineering from mapped data elements. Train candidate models (gradient boosting, logistic regression, neural network) on historical HF admissions with 30-day readmission outcome. k-fold cross-validation, hyperparameter tuning, and fairness/bias evaluation across demographic subgroups. Target: AUC > 0.75, calibrated risk deciles.

Deliverable: Validated model registered in Snowflake Model Registry with performance metrics, feature importance, and subgroup analysis.

Phase 4: Model Deployment for Inference

Deploy the trained model for real-time inference at two critical trigger points: index admission (initial HF admission risk stratification) and index discharge (discharge readmission risk score). Snowflake model serving via Snowpark Container Services or warehouse-based inference. Scoring pipeline ingests live EHR data, generates patient-level risk scores, and writes results to a risk score table accessible by downstream systems.

Deliverable: Production scoring pipeline with real-time risk scores at admission and discharge, monitoring dashboard, and model drift detection.

Phase 5: Epic Hyperspace Integration

Integrate risk scores and clinical recommendations directly into Epic Hyperspace patient charts via the Snowflake–Epic integration. Risk scores surface in the patient storyboard, BPA (Best Practice Advisory) alerts fire for high-risk patients, and discharge planning workflows incorporate model-driven recommendations. Leverage Epic CDS Hooks and/or Snowflake External Functions to deliver scores in real time within the clinician's native workflow.

Deliverable: Risk score display in Epic patient chart, BPA alerts for high-risk patients, discharge recommendation panel, and clinician feedback loop for model improvement.
